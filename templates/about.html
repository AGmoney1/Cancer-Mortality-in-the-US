Webpage Context:

This webpage investigates how cancer mortality rates have changed from 1999-2020. Specifically, this page looks into the mortality of the 4 most deadly cancers: colon and rectum, lung and bronchus, female breast, and prostate.

For me, mortality surrounding cancer has always been a topic of interest for me as cancer gravely affected both of my paternal grandparents. This project gave me the lens to investigate cancer mortality over time from an analytical perspective, allowing me to gain insight on cancer mortality throughout America since 1999.

Data Pipeline:

1. Go to the CDC Wonder database and click on Cancer Statistics

2. Select the parameters that will narrow down the data

3. Copy the data into a Google Sheets, and remove any excess cells

4. Export that data as a .csv (comma separated value) file, and upload that file to a data folder

5. Create and run a python code that converts the .csv file into dictionary which is stored in a .json file. Now all the data is in a format that is easy to access.

6. Go back to the CDC Wonder database and click on Underlying Cause of Death

7. Repeat steps 2-5 with data on death and population. Now all the data is organized and ready to be used.

Vocabulary:

Cancer: a disease caused by an uncontrolled division of abnormal cells in a part of the body.

Colon cancer (also known as cororectal cancer): A growth of cells that forms in the lower end of the digestive tract. Most of these cancers start as noncancerous growths called polyps.

Lung and bronchus cancer: Damaged cells dividing uncontrollably create masses, or tumors, of tissue that eventually keep your lungs from working properly.

Female breast cancer: A disease in which damaged cells in the breast grow out of control.

Prostate cancer: When cells in the prostate gland start to grow out of control. The prostate is a gland found only in males.

Mortality rate: A measure of the number of deaths in a particular population, scaled to the size of that population.

Peer Feedback:

I received feedback from three of my peers. Each piece of feedback was able to identify that the map would show my investigation into how cancer affects each state different, and the line graph would show details about the types of data. The constructive criticism was broader, with no repetitions. The main takeaways from the constructive criticism were to add more color to both of my graphs, and to incorporate some aspect of qualitative text on with both graphs.

Takeaways:

From the feedback, and a little self-criticism, I decided to modify my line graph a little. I changed it from a graph simply showing the amount of deaths by cancer each year, to a graph that showed how the number of deaths by different cancer types composed the total amount of deaths by cancer each year. This choice allowed me to incorporate a smoother use of color, and to demonstate my data in a much more streamlined way.
